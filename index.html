SYN[i]

Synesthesia is a perceptual phenomenon in which the stimulation of one sense automatically triggers experiences in another.

A sound may appear as a color, a letter may carry a specific hue, or a number may feel spatial or textured.

These cross-sensory connections happen naturally and consistently, forming a unique inner world for each person who experiences them.

Synesthesia is not a disorder, but a different way of perceiving reality—one in which the boundaries between the senses gently blur.

Synesthesia is not a disorder, but a different way of perceiving reality—one in which the boundaries between the senses gently blur.

Written language is experienced as form rather than symbol. For some synesthetes, letters are not abstract signs but spatial entities with weight, direction, and geometry. Language becomes something shaped and seen before it is understood.

For some synesthetes, color is an inseparable part of language. Letters do not appear as neutral marks, but arrive with their own chromatic presence. A word is already tinted before it is read, and its color remains stable, familiar, and personal.
